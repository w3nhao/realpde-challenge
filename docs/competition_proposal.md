## 1 Competition description

### 1.1 Background and impact

The prediction of the evolution of complex physical systems is a fundamental challenge in both science and engineering. These systems exhibit complex spatiotemporal dynamics, leading to their high-dimensional, strongly nonlinear, tightly coupled, and unsteady nature. Consequently, performance on such systems serves as a stringent test of model capability across modern AI paradigms, including large foundation models [24, 14] and generative models [16, 8]. The inherent difficulty and distinctive characteristics of this task also give rise to a dedicated research field, Scientific Machine Learning (Scientific ML). This area focuses on the principled design of algorithms tailored for the prediction of complex physical systems, spanning applications such as fluid dynamics [4, 10], combustion [9], and controlled nuclear fusion [19].

In the domain of complex physical system modeling, Scientific ML also emerges as a promising direction. Traditionally, numerical solvers rely on the underlying partial differential equations (PDEs) governing the physical system and employ discretization schemes such as the finite element method (FEM) [27, 5] or finite difference method (FDM) [3, 22]. These approaches typically require numerous iterative updates to converge, resulting in substantial computational cost. In contrast, Scientific ML methods [12, 7] approximate the solution operator with neural networks, enabling the prediction of system states through a single forward pass and thereby significantly accelerating the solution process.

However, the development of Scientific ML still faces several pressing challenges. One critical issue is the lack of experimentally observed ***real-world data*** [21, 9]. Most existing approaches train and evaluate their models primarily on *simulated data* generated by traditional numerical solvers. This hinders the practical deployment and rigorous real-world validation of these methods, as a substantial gap often exists between simulated and real-world data distributions. Real-world data provide faithful measurements of physical systems by avoiding numerical errors and simplified physics. However, they are often costly to collect, noisy, and limited in observability, with experimental conditions (e.g., inflow uniformity) that are difficult to strictly control and measurement noise (e.g., camera noise) that can introduce errors. In contrast, simulated data are relatively cheaper to generate and can offer broader modalities (variables of the physical system) together with dense coverage over parameter spaces, but they may suffer from numerical inaccuracies arising from modeling approximations and discretization errors.

Given the substantial gap between real-world data and simulated data, as well as the need for real-world deployment, it is crucial to develop an evaluation framework that is grounded in real-world data and closely reflects practical scenarios. Accordingly, we design this competition based on the requirements of real-world deployment. This competition offers a timely and high-impact evaluation for the NeurIPS community by assessing Scientific ML under settings that closely resemble real-world scenarios. By centering evaluation on real-world data and realistic inference settings, it directly addresses a key bottleneck that limits the practical adoption of neural PDE solvers, namely the gap between simulated data and real-world observations. The resulting tasks and metrics encourage models that are not only accurate but also safe, efficient, robust, and physically plausible under distribution shifts, aligning closely with NeurIPS priorities in generalization, representation learning, and reliable machine learning. Beyond the competition itself, we expect the released dataset, evaluation protocol, and baselines to serve as a durable community resource, fostering reproducible comparison and accelerating progress toward deployable Scientific ML algorithms.

### 1.2 Novelty

As the first Scientific ML competition at NeurIPS that evaluates on data from real physical systems, this competition is dedicated to advancing the deployment of Scientific ML in real physical systems and to driving progress in the broader research community. It introduces unique innovations along the following three aspects.

- **Data.** We release a new dataset consisting of paired real-world trajectories and their corresponding simulated trajectories. It focuses on cross-sectional flow data over an airfoil, extracted from 3D simulations and real experiments. This setting is a canonical scenario in aerodynamic analysis and wing design.

- **Tasks.** The competition features two complementary tracks that target two key core capabilities for deployable Scientific ML. The Simulation to Real-world transfer learning (Sim2Real) track focuses on bridging the gap between simulated data and real-world data for foil dynamics. The Long-Term Test-Time Adaptation (LTTTA) track focuses on long-horizon autoregressive prediction with continuous adaptation to streaming real-world observations.

- **Metrics.** The competition introduces a physics-aware and deployment-oriented evaluation protocol. First, instead of relying only on point-wise errors, the metrics also assess physically meaningful quantities, including turbulent kinetic energy and lift/drag errors, with an additional mean velocity profile error for long-horizon evaluation. Second, efficiency is measured by a sigmoid-shaped normalized time metric relative to traditional numerical solvers, yielding a smooth score that remains high below a runtime threshold while increasingly penalizing slower models once the threshold is exceeded. Third, it emphasizes reliability by introducing a safe prediction score that jointly evaluates physical accuracy, uncertainty coverage, and interval tightness, and further combines safety and efficiency into a composite score for final ranking.

### 1.3 Data

We collect a new dataset based on the NACA4418 airfoil, as it is a practically relevant and widely studied foil geometry with close connections to real-world aerodynamic and hydrodynamic applications, such as wing-section analysis, blade design, and flow optimization [17, 15, 13]. The paired real-world and simulated data are collected under aligned experimental and numerical protocols. In the real-world setting, a three-dimensional airfoil is installed in a circulating water tunnel, and the cross-sectional flow field is measured using time-resolved Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) [1]. The flow is seeded with tracer particles and illuminated by a laser sheet, while high-speed cameras record the particle motion to reconstruct the velocity field on the measurement plane. In the numerical setting, we perform matched 3D CFD simulations under the same geometric configuration and operating conditions as the experiments, and extract the corresponding cross-sectional flow fields for comparison. As for the operating conditions, we vary the angle of attack and Reynolds number to cover diverse operating regimes, so that the resulting dataset contains paired real-world and simulated trajectories under consistent conditions. ### 1.4 Tasks and application scenarios

This competition features two distinct yet complementary technical tracks: Simulation to Real-world transfer learning (Sim2Real) and Long-Term Test-Time Adaptation (LTTTA). Both of them are centered on the Foil dataset from the RealPDEBench [9], which is a dataset of practical significance given its direct relevance to critical engineering applications such as aircraft foils and wind turbine blades [2, 6]. In these scenarios, the precise modeling of fluid dynamics is indispensable for optimizing performance, efficiency, and structural safety. The Foil dataset provides paired real-world measured and simulated data capturing cross-sectional fluid dynamics of a section of 3D NACA4418 foil, encompassing diverse angles-of-attack ($0°$, $5°$, $10°$, $15°$, $20°$) and Reynolds numbers (2968--17031) to reflect the varied operating conditions of foil-based engineering systems. The examples of both the simulated data and real-world data are illustrated in Figure XXXX.

The first track, the Sim2Real track, addresses the core challenge of bridging the inherent gap between numerical simulated data and real-world measured data for fluid dynamics of foil. Simulated data for the Foil dataset, generated via Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) solvers like Waterlily [23] on GPUs, offer advantages such as complete modalities (velocity fields $u$, $v$, and pressure $p$) and dense parameter coverage, yet suffer from numerical errors due to discretization and modeling simplifications [18, 11]. In contrast, real-world data, which is measured via the Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) in circulating water tunnels capturing realistic 3D Fluid-Structure Interaction (FSI) and small-scale vortex structures but is limited to measured modalities ($u$, $v$) and contaminated by measurement noise [20]. Participants in this track are tasked with developing innovative machine learning models that effectively fuse the strengths of these two data types to enhance the accuracy of modeling real-world fluid dynamics of foil. It includes strategies to leverage the rich modal information and parameter diversity of simulated data while adapting to the noise and observational constraints of real-world measurements, delivering models that generalize robustly to real-world fluid dynamics. The Sim2Real track directly addresses industrial demands, such as optimizing aircraft foil aerodynamics to reduce drag and enhancing wind turbine blade efficiency to maximize energy capture. In practical scenarios, the real-world experimental data is costly and partially observed, necessitating the smart integration of simulated data to build reliable surrogate models.

The second track, the LTTTA track, focuses on the challenge of long-horizon predictive modeling for foil systems with online adaptation to new real-time observational data. Long-term adaptation is crucial for models as it enables continuous integration of real-time observations during multi-step autoregressive prediction [26]. It can effectively mitigates error accumulation and dynamically adapting to the complex 3D effects, turbulence, and unsteady dynamics of fluid systems; thereby sustaining predictive reliability over extended time horizons. In this track, participants design machine learning models capable of iterative multi-step autoregressive prediction with continuous adaptation: the model first predicts the evolution of the foil's surrounding fluid fields and forces over a future N-step horizon (from time $t$ to $t + N$) using initial state information (e.g., velocity fields, lift, and drag). Subsequently, the model is provided with the real-world measured observation at time $t + 1$ (captured via PIV and sensing technologies), which it must utilize to refine its understanding of the unsteady FSI dynamics before performing a new N-step prediction from time $t + 1$ to $t + 1 + N$. This iterative process repeats over time, with the model incorporating new observations to counteract error accumulation, adapt to unmodeled variations in unsteady flow, and maintain predictive accuracy across extended horizons. This track mimics real-world operational scenarios where foil-based systems generate incremental sensor data, such as real-time monitoring of airflow during flight or wind turbine blade performance in changing wind speeds, where models must make continuous long-horizon predictions to support control decisions and maintenance planning.

Together, the two tracks target key technical bottlenecks in scientific ML for fluid dynamics. They focus on the effective integration of simulated and real-world data, and the development of adaptive models capable of reliable long-term prediction, advancing the state-of-the-art machine learning methods in modeling real-world complex fluid dynamics.

**Physical metrics.** The physical metrics that can reflect the model's capture of physical properties beyond point by point accuracy. Here, the physical metrics are designed for two tracks separately. Firstly, both tracks have two types: Turbulent Kinetic Energy (TKE) error and lift-drag error (LDE).

1. Turbulent Kinetic Energy (TKE) error is directly tied to physical metrics because it is a core measure of the energy contained in fluid velocity fluctuations. By comparing the statistical variance of predicted and reference velocity components (u, v) in real physical space, making it a physically interpretable metric for validating fluid dynamics models against real-world flow behavior.

$$TKE_{\text{rel L2}} = \frac{\|e - \hat{e}\|_2}{\|e\|_2}, \quad e = \frac{\overline{(u')^2} + \overline{(v')^2}}{2}, \quad \overline{(u')^2} = \frac{1}{T} \sum_t \left( u(t) - \bar{u} \right)^2,$$

where $u$ and $v$ are two channels of velocities in x- and y- directions, respectively.

2. Lift-drag error: The foil placed in the fluid will affect the mode of the flow, resulting in wake and vortex, and the changed flow field will also have a reverse effect on the foil. The influence of the flow field on the structure can be reflected through changes in lift and drag, which is known as fluid-structure interaction (FSI), a complex and important problem in fluid mechanics. The difference between the predicted force and the observed value can demonstrate the model's learning of the overall forces.

$$CD_{\text{rel L2}} = \frac{\|C_D - \hat{C}_D\|_2}{\|C_D\|_2}, \quad CL_{\text{rel L2}} = \frac{\|C_L - \hat{C}_L\|_2}{\|C_L\|_2}$$

where $C_D$ and $C_L$ are the coefficients of drag and lift.

Secondly, for the track "long-term autoregressive evaluation", we design an additional metric that evaluates the long-term simulation ability of models: Mean Velocity Profile Error (MVPE).

In the existing numerical studies of fluid literature, the mean velocity profile (MVP) is a generally employed summary statistic to measure discrepancies between simulated data and real-world data. For example, there is a significant decrease of $u$ near the wake region behind the foil. We select different probes positioned at $(x_{\text{probe},j}, y_{\text{probe},j})_{j=1}^{N_{\text{probe}}}$ to calculate the differences between the time-average velocity field of real-world data and model predictions, forming the MVPE:

$$MVPE_{\text{rel L2}} = \frac{\left\| \frac{1}{N_{\text{probe}}} \sum_j \left( \bar{u}(x_{\text{probe},j}, y_{\text{probe},j}) - \hat{\bar{u}}(x_{\text{probe},j}, y_{\text{probe},j}) \right)^2 \right\|_2}{\left\| \frac{1}{N_{\text{probe}}} \sum_j \left( \bar{u}(x_{\text{probe},j}, y_{\text{probe},j}) \right)^2 \right\|_2}$$

**Time Metrics.** Let $t_{\text{neural}}$ denote the inference time of the neural PDE solver and $t_{\text{numerical}}$ denote the runtime of the traditional numerical solver. We first define the normalized runtime ratio as

$$r = \frac{t_{\text{neural}}}{t_{\text{numerical}}}.$$

Based on this ratio, we define the time score (ST) using a sigmoid-shaped function:

$$ST = \frac{1}{1 + \left(\frac{r}{r_{\min}}\right)^{1/2}},$$

where $r_{\min}$ is a scale parameter controlling the transition point of the score. This formulation assigns high scores to models whose normalized runtime ratio is smaller than $r_{\min}$, while smoothly penalizing slower methods as the normalized runtime increases. The square-root exponent ($\beta = 1/2$) makes the decay milder than a standard linear power, preventing the score from dropping too aggressively. As a result, the metric provides a smooth and normalized measure of inference efficiency relative to numerical solvers.

**Safe Prediction Score.** The Safe Prediction Score ($SPS$) quantifies the "safe" prediction by penalizing missing ground truth (unsafe intervals) and rewarding accurate, tight intervals. It is computed per sample and averaged across all test samples, and works as a wrapper for Physical Metrics ($PM$), which can be $TKE_{\text{rel L2}}$, $CD_{\text{rel L2}}$, $CL_{\text{rel L2}}$, and $MVPE_{\text{rel L2}}$, and their related ground truth:

- If ground truth $y$ is not in the interval $[L, U]$: Score = -penalty<0.
- If ground truth $y$ is in the interval $[L, U]$:

$$SPS_{PM} = (1 - PM) \times \exp(-\alpha \times \text{Normalized Interval Length})$$

where $\alpha$ and penalty **can be different across different** $PM$ **and different test time.**

Normalized Interval Length:

$$\text{Normalized Interval Length} = \frac{U - L}{\sigma_{\text{global}}}$$

where $\sigma_{\text{global}}$ is the global standard deviation of the target variable (precomputed for each dataset to ensure normalization across physics scenarios).

$$SPS = \beta_1 SPS_{TKE} + \beta_2 SPS_{CD} + \beta_3 SPS_{CL} + \beta_4 SPS_{MVPE}$$

**Composite Score.** The composite score of a sample is calculated as: TODO:

$$\text{Composite Score} = \begin{cases} SPS \times p_1 & \text{if SPS} < threshold \\ (SPS \times p_1) + ST \times p_2 & \text{if SPS} \geq threshold \end{cases}$$

where $p_1 + p_2 = 1$. The overall score is calculated as the sum of the composite scores of all samples.

## 2 Organizational aspects

### 2.1 Protocol

The competition will be hosted on the Codabench platform [25], ensuring a unified, transparent, and reproducible evaluation process for all participants. To participate, teams must first create an account on the platform and download the official starting kit, which includes Jupyter notebooks, data access guidelines, baseline model implementations, and submission templates. Participants are required to prepare their submission code in compliance with the specified interfaces and requirements outlined in the starting kit, which includes ensuring compatibility with the PyTorch framework, adhering to data loading standards via the dataset module, and following the training/evaluation pipeline specifications. Once prepared, submissions are uploaded to Codabench, where they will be trained and evaluated on dedicated GPU clusters. The evaluation leverages a modular code framework integrated with the core components of RealPDEBench, including the dataset, task configurations, and evaluation pipelines. To prevent overfitting to public test data, participants' models will be assessed on both the provided validation set and a private test set with similar characteristics but unseen parameter regimes (e.g., novel angles-of-attack or Reynolds numbers). After evaluation, participants will receive a detailed performance report including scores across all metrics, alongside their ranking on the public leaderboard.

### 2.2 Rules and Engagement

This challenge starts on June 15, 2026 and ends on October 15, 2026.

- This challenge is open to all individuals and academic/industrial teams worldwide, with each team limited to a maximum of 3 members.
- The competition features two independent tracks (Sim-to-Real Track and Long-term Autoregression Track) running simultaneously; participants may choose to compete in one track or both tracks based on their research focus.
- The competition consists of three phases: Warm-up Phase, Development Phase, and Final Phase.
- During the Warm-up Phase and Development Phase, participants can submit their codes and view their real-time results on the dedicated leaderboard for each track.
- Organizers reserve the right to adjust the global scoring formula during the Warm-up Phase based on participant feedback to ensure fairness and alignment with competition objectives.
- Organizers may release additional baseline results or technical insights during the competition to stimulate innovation and facilitate meaningful comparisons across submissions.
- Each team (using a single group account linked to a shared email address) is limited to 10 submissions per day and 500 total submissions per phase across all participating tracks.
- Multiple accounts per team or individual are strictly prohibited; any violation will result in disqualification.
- Organizers strongly encourage participants to make their submission codes publicly available (anonymized versions are permitted during the competition) to foster knowledge sharing and community collaboration.
- Organizers will announce the start/end of each phase via the official mailing list. Key updates, FAQs, and clarifications will be synced to the competition website as a centralized information hub.
- Participants can communicate and provide feedback through the forum in Codabench, and the organizers will continue to root in the forum content.
- The final ranking for each track will be determined solely by the global error, calculated based on the all weighted metrics, and the results will be notified to all participants via email and the competition website.
- To be eligible for prizes in any track, winning teams must agree to open-source their complete solution (including model architectures, training scripts, hyperparameters, and key implementation details) no later than two weeks before the NeurIPS results presentation event.

### 2.3 Schedule and readiness

The competition spans a total of 19 weeks, divided into three phases to balance exploration, development, and validation:

**Warm-up Phase (5 weeks):** This phase allows participants to familiarize themselves with the Foil dataset, starting kit, and Codabench platform. Participants can submit initial models to test compatibility, provide feedback on technical issues, and gain insights into baseline performance. Organizers will address feedback promptly, adjust evaluation criteria if necessary, and release supplementary resources (e.g., tutorial webinars) to support onboarding.

**Development Phase (10 weeks):** The core phase for model development, where participants refine their approaches using the provided training and validation data. Teams can leverage their own computing resources for iterative testing and then submit models to Codabench for official evaluation on the public test set. Organizers will periodically update the leaderboard and may release additional baseline results to stimulate competition and highlight key performance milestones.

**Final Phase (4 weeks):** During this phase, organizers validate the top-performing models from the development phase. All final submissions undergo re-training on the competition's GPU clusters to ensure reproducibility, followed by evaluation on the private test set to determine the final rankings. Organizers will also verify code integrity, check for compliance with competition rules, and conduct robustness tests (e.g., sensitivity to data noise) for the top solutions.

Key milestones include:

- Competition launch: June 15, 2026;
- Warm-up phase conclusion: July 19, 2026 (5 weeks);
- Development phase conclusion: September 27, 2026 (10 weeks);
- Final phase conclusion: October 25, 2026 (4 weeks)
- Announcement of final results: November 10, 2026;
- Winning teams' code and fact sheet submission deadline: November 25, 2026 (2 weeks before the conference presentation);
- Results presentation at the associated conference/workshop: December 6, 2026 (aligned with typical NeurIPS workshop timelines in early December)

### 2.4 Competition promotion and incentives

To maximize the visibility and reach of the competition, we will leverage a multi-channel promotion strategy covering top academic venues, professional communities, and industry platforms. Specifically, announcements will be made at leading machine learning and engineering conferences (including NeurIPS, ICML, AAAI) as well as specialized workshops focused on the intersection of machine learning and physical sciences, ensuring engagement with researchers and practitioners in both domains. We will also distribute calls for participation through relevant academic mailing lists, social media platforms (such as Twitter/X, Wechat, and ResearchGate) to tap into broader technical and industrial audiences. For incentives, attractive monetary prizes will be awarded to the top three teams in each track: 5000 USD for first place, 3000 USD for second place, and 1000 USD for third place in each track, recognizing their innovative contributions to solve the challenges of modeling complex real-world fluid dynamics. Beyond cash rewards, winning teams will be invited to deliver in-depth presentations of their solutions in a dedicated webinar, offering a platform to showcase their work to the global scientific machine learning and AI for Engineering communities. Additionally, all winning teams will be invited to co-author a joint paper summarizing the competition results, key insights, and state-of-the-art advancements, which will be submitted to a relevant academic journal to enhance the impact and visibility of their research.
